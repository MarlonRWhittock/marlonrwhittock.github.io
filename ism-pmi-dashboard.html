ISM PMI & NMI
Economic Activity Analysis

The Institute for Supply Management (ISM) publishes two monthly surveys that serve as the most widely followed leading indicators of U.S. economic activity. The Manufacturing PMI tracks the goods-producing sector through purchasing manager responses on new orders, production, employment, deliveries, and inventories. The Non-Manufacturing NMI (Services) covers the other 68% of the economy — everything from healthcare and finance to technology and construction. Together, these reports tell you where GDP, corporate earnings, employment, and inflation are headed before the official data confirms it. A reading above 50 signals expansion; below 50 signals contraction. The further from 50, the stronger the signal.

Key Observations & Analysis
📈
Manufacturing Recovery Is Real, But Fragile
The ISM Manufacturing PMI printed 52.4 in February 2026, marking the second consecutive month above 50 after a prolonged contraction that lasted from March through December 2025 (10 straight months below 50). This is the first back-to-back expansion since October 2022. The recovery is being driven by a surge in new orders (55.8) and production (53.5), suggesting genuine demand improvement rather than just inventory noise. However, this recovery comes with a major asterisk: prices are surging (70.5) due to tariff impacts, employment is still contracting (48.8), and supplier delivery times are lengthening (55.1). The data paints a picture of an economy where demand is coming back, but supply chains are under stress and manufacturers are not yet confident enough to hire aggressively.
55.8
New Orders
New Orders Signal Sustained Expansion Ahead
New Orders at 55.8 is the most important leading indicator in the PMI suite. It tells you where demand is headed before it shows up in production or revenue. After spending most of 2024–2025 below 50, New Orders have now been above 50 for two months running (57.1 in Jan, 55.8 in Feb). The 3-month average has risen to 53.5 from a 6-month average of 51.0, confirming a genuine trend shift rather than a one-month blip. This means manufacturers are receiving more orders than they can immediately fill, which should translate into higher production and eventually more hiring over the next 2–3 months. The slight moderation from January (-1.3 pts) is normal after a sharp initial surge and does not signal a reversal.
53.5
Production
Production Ramping Up — Confirms Order Strength
Production at 53.5 confirms that the new orders are being converted into actual output. After bottoming at 44.3 in April 2025, production has recovered steadily and is now firmly in expansion territory for the second straight month. When you see both New Orders and Production above 50 simultaneously, it validates that the expansion is real — factories are not just booking orders but actually running lines. The 3-month average of 53.5 versus the 6-month average of 51.8 shows accelerating momentum. This is the kind of setup that typically precedes GDP revisions upward, as manufacturing output feeds directly into the industrial production component of GDP.
48.8
Employment
Employment: The Lagging Piece — But Turning
Employment at 48.8 remains below 50, meaning manufacturers are still shedding jobs on net. But context matters: this is improving from a cycle low of 43.4 in July 2024, and the trajectory is clearly upward (3-month avg 47.3, up from 6-month avg 46.2). Employment is a leading indicator because manufacturers hire when they believe the order pipeline is sustainable. The fact that hiring hasn't caught up to order strength yet tells you companies are cautious — they're running overtime and using temp labor before committing to permanent headcount. Historically, when New Orders sustain above 55 for 2–3 months, Employment follows above 50 within 1–2 quarters. Watch for a cross above 50 by Q2 2026 as a confirmation that the recovery has legs.
38.8
Cust. Inv.
Inventories: Destocking Nearing End — Restocking Cycle Ahead
Inventories at 48.8 (below 50 = destocking) tells you manufacturers have been running down stockpiles for months. But Customer Inventories at 38.8 is the real story — this is extremely low and one of the lowest readings in years. When your customers' shelves are this bare and new orders are surging, it creates the conditions for a powerful restocking cycle. Manufacturers will need to rebuild both their own inventories and replenish their customers' supply. This restocking dynamic can add 1–2 percentage points to GDP growth and tends to sustain manufacturing expansion for multiple quarters. It's one of the most bullish signals in the current data set.
70.5
Prices
Prices Surging to 70.5 — Tariff-Driven Inflation Risk
Prices Paid spiked +11.5 points in a single month to 70.5, the highest since mid-2022. This is a flashing warning sign for inflation. The surge is overwhelmingly tariff-driven: Section 232 tariffs on steel and aluminum have made domestically produced commodities among the most expensive in the world. Manufacturers report that raw materials like steel, wire, and semiconductors must now be sourced domestically at significantly higher costs. When Prices Paid is above 60, it historically correlates with PPI acceleration, and above 70 it begins feeding into CPI. This is the single biggest risk to the recovery — if cost pressures squeeze margins too hard, manufacturers may slow hiring and investment even as demand improves. For the Fed, this complicates any rate cut narrative and likely keeps policy on hold.
55.1
Deliveries
Supplier Deliveries Slowing — Bottleneck Watch
Supplier Deliveries at 55.1 means deliveries are slowing (above 50 = longer wait times). This has risen for four consecutive months from 49.3 in September. Slowing deliveries can mean two things: either demand is outstripping supply capacity (bullish for growth), or supply disruptions are creating bottlenecks (bearish for margins). In this case, it's both. Tariff-driven sourcing shifts and semiconductor shortages are creating genuine bottlenecks, while the demand recovery is straining capacity. This is a classic late-recovery dynamic where the expansion is self-reinforcing but also self-limiting — if deliveries keep slowing toward 60+, it signals capacity constraints that could choke off growth.

Understanding PMI Components as Leading Indicators
The ISM Manufacturing PMI is composed of sub-indices that act as leading indicators for the broader economy. When these components move above or below 50, they signal where manufacturing — and by extension GDP, corporate earnings, and employment — is headed in the coming months. New Orders leads Production by 1–2 months. Production leads Employment by 1–2 months. Inventories signal where the business cycle is in the destocking/restocking process. Together, they tell a story about the direction and sustainability of economic growth before it shows up in official GDP data.
New Orders ● LEADING INDICATOR
What it measures: The volume of new orders placed with manufacturers. This is the single most important leading indicator in the PMI suite because orders must be placed before anything is produced, shipped, or paid for.

Current reading: 55.8 — New Orders surged to 57.1 in January and held strong at 55.8 in February, the first back-to-back readings above 55 since early 2022. The 3-month average (53.5) is now decisively above the 6-month average (51.0), confirming a genuine trend shift. After spending 15 of the prior 18 months below 50, this breakout signals that demand is returning to manufacturing in a meaningful way. Companies are reporting increased RFPs and new contracts, particularly in electronics, food & beverage, and defense-related industries.

What it means going forward: Sustained New Orders above 55 historically leads to Production acceleration within 1–2 months and Employment recovery within 1–2 quarters. It also signals rising industrial production and positive GDP contributions from manufacturing. Watch for whether orders hold above 55 or begin to normalize — some of the current strength may reflect tariff front-loading as companies lock in prices ahead of additional tariff rounds.
Production ● LEADING INDICATOR
What it measures: The rate of output at manufacturing facilities. Production follows New Orders and leads Employment — when factories ramp up output, they first use existing capacity and overtime before hiring new workers.

Current reading: 53.5 — Production recovered sharply from a cycle low of 44.3 in April 2025, hitting 55.9 in January before moderating to 53.5 in February. The moderation is partly seasonal and partly reflects supply chain constraints (Supplier Deliveries slowing to 55.1). What matters most is that Production is tracking New Orders higher — when both are above 50 simultaneously, it validates that the expansion is real. Manufacturers are converting orders into output, not just booking business they can't fulfill.

What it means going forward: Rising Production feeds directly into Industrial Production (a key GDP input) and signals improving capacity utilization. As production sustains above 50, manufacturers will exhaust spare capacity and begin facing hire-or-lose-orders decisions, which is the catalyst for Employment to follow above 50. The gap between New Orders (55.8) and Production (53.5) also suggests more room for output to expand.
Employment ● LEADING INDICATOR
What it measures: Whether manufacturers are adding or reducing headcount. Employment is a leading indicator for the broader labor market and consumer spending — manufacturing hiring decisions ripple through local economies and supply chains.

Current reading: 48.8 — Still below 50, meaning the sector is shedding jobs on net. But the trajectory is clearly improving: up from a devastating cycle low of 43.4 in July 2024, and the 3-month average (47.3) is above the 6-month average (46.2). Manufacturers are cautious — they're working existing employees harder (overtime, temp labor) rather than committing to permanent hires. Industry comments confirm this: Food & Beverage reports being "inundated with applicants" for the first time in five years, while other sectors report slow, deliberate hiring plans.

What it means going forward: Historically, when New Orders sustain above 55 for 2–3 consecutive months, Employment crosses above 50 within 1–2 quarters. If the current order trend holds, expect Employment to reach 50+ by Q2–Q3 2026. This would be a powerful positive signal for the labor market, consumer confidence, and the consumer spending component of GDP (68%). Until then, the below-50 reading acts as a drag on the overall recovery narrative.
Inventories ● LEADING INDICATOR
What it measures: Whether manufacturers are building or drawing down their own stockpiles. Inventories are a leading indicator for the business cycle — the shift from destocking to restocking is one of the most powerful GDP accelerators.

Current reading: 48.8 (Own Inventories) / 38.8 (Customer Inventories) — Own Inventories at 48.8 means manufacturers are still running down stockpiles, but the pace is slowing (up from 42.6 in October). Customer Inventories at 38.8 is the critical number here — it's extremely low and means your customers' shelves are nearly bare. When you combine surging New Orders (55.8) with depleted Customer Inventories (38.8), you get the preconditions for a restocking cycle: customers need to replenish their stocks, manufacturers need to rebuild their own, and this creates a multiplier effect on production and ordering.

What it means going forward: The restocking cycle is one of the most reliable GDP boosters in economics. It can add 1–2 percentage points to GDP growth and typically sustains manufacturing expansion for 2–4 quarters. Watch for Own Inventories to cross above 50 (signaling the restocking has begun) — this would confirm the expansion is self-sustaining and has multiple quarters of runway.

Order Backlog & Prices: Demand vs. Cost Pressure
Order Backlog and Prices Paid together tell you whether the expansion is healthy or overheating. Rising backlogs with moderate prices = healthy demand. Rising backlogs with surging prices = demand is outstripping supply capacity, creating inflation risk and margin compression.
Order Backlog
Current: 56.6 — Surged from 39.6 in December to 51.6 in January and 56.6 in February. This is the highest backlog reading since the post-COVID restocking boom. Rising backlogs confirm that demand is exceeding current production capacity, which supports the case for sustained output growth and eventually more hiring. However, combined with slowing Supplier Deliveries, it also signals that supply chains are struggling to keep up.
Prices Paid
Current: 70.5 — The biggest single-month jump (+11.5 pts) in years. Tariffs on steel, aluminum, and imported components are the primary driver. As one Transportation Equipment manufacturer put it, domestically produced steel and aluminum are now the most expensive in the world. When Prices Paid sustains above 65, it historically feeds into PPI and eventually CPI within 3–6 months. This is the biggest risk to the recovery and the main reason the Fed will likely stay on hold.
Supply Chain & Trade
These indicators track delivery times, export/import activity, and the overall health of global supply chains. They're particularly important right now because tariff policy is reshaping trade flows and creating new bottlenecks.
Supplier Deliveries
Current: 55.1 (above 50 = slower deliveries) — Four consecutive months of slowing. Semiconductor shortages and tariff-driven sourcing changes are the main culprits. When deliveries slow while orders rise, it means the supply side can't keep pace with demand, which reinforces both the inflation and the sustained-demand narratives.
Exports
Current: 50.3 — Just barely in expansion after spending most of 2025 below 50 (bottoming at 40.1 in May). The recovery is fragile and heavily dependent on trade policy. Tariff uncertainty makes export contracts risky for both parties. Any escalation could push this back below 50 quickly.
Imports
Current: 54.9 — Imports surging to 54.9 from 44.6 in December is notable. This likely reflects tariff front-loading: companies are rushing to import materials before additional tariff rounds take effect. This artificially inflates near-term activity and could lead to a pullback once stockpiling is complete. Watch for a sharp reversal in 2–3 months as a sign the front-loading effect is fading.

Key Observations & Analysis
🚀
Services Economy Is Accelerating Broadly
The ISM Services NMI surged to 56.1 in February 2026, the highest reading since February 2023 and a +5.9 point jump from January's 50.2. This is a dramatic acceleration, not a gradual recovery. The breadth is equally impressive: 14 of 18 services sectors are now in growth, up from 11 in January. Services represent approximately 68% of U.S. GDP, so when this sector accelerates, it moves the needle on the entire economy. The surge is being driven by a powerful combination of strong demand (New Orders 58.6, Business Activity 59.9), rebuilding backlogs (Order Backlog +11.9 pts to 55.9), and surging services exports (57.2). Crucially, services inflation is actually decelerating (Prices Paid fell to 63.0 from 66.6), which is the opposite of what's happening in manufacturing. This gives the economy growth without adding to the Fed's inflation problem.
Business Activity Near 60
59.9
Business Activity at 59.9 is the services equivalent of Production in manufacturing — it measures actual output. Readings near 60 historically correlate with GDP growth above 3%. The acceleration from 57.4 to 59.9 shows the expansion is gaining speed, not plateauing. This is being driven by AI/data center investment (Construction, IT, Utilities all in strong expansion), healthcare demand, and consumer-facing services. When Business Activity sustains above 55, it feeds directly into the PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures) component of GDP, which the Fed watches closely.
Demand Pipeline Is Deep
58.6 NO
New Orders jumped +5.5 pts to 58.6, but the real standout is Order Backlog surging +11.9 pts to 55.9 from just 44.0 in January. When both are elevated simultaneously, it means not only is new demand strong, but companies are building a queue of work they haven't been able to deliver yet. This provides visibility into future Business Activity — even if new orders slow somewhat, the backlog provides a buffer that should keep output elevated for months. Services Exports at 57.2 (up from 45.0) confirms this is not just domestic demand; U.S. services are gaining share internationally.
Employment Crossing 50
51.8
Services Employment at 51.8 is now above 50 for two consecutive months (50.3 in Jan, 51.8 in Feb), the highest since June 2024. Unlike manufacturing where employers are still cautious, services firms are beginning to add headcount. This is critical because services employ roughly 80% of the private sector workforce. When services employers start hiring, it supports consumer confidence, wage growth, and spending — creating a positive feedback loop. The acceleration from 46.4 in July 2025 to 51.8 now represents a complete turnaround in less than 8 months.
Inflation Decelerating
63.0 ▼
While Manufacturing Prices are surging to 70.5 (tariff-driven), Services Prices Paid declined to 63.0 from 66.6 — the largest single-month drop in the current cycle. This is exactly what the Fed and the market want to see: services inflation decelerating while growth accelerates. Services inflation has been the stickier component of CPI, so any deceleration here is meaningful for the overall inflation outlook. If this trend continues, it strengthens the case for eventual rate cuts, even as manufacturing inflation remains elevated. The divergence between services (decelerating) and goods (surging) creates a complex but net-positive picture for the economy.

Understanding NMI Components
The Services NMI components work similarly to manufacturing but measure the much larger services economy. Business Activity is the services equivalent of Production. New Orders, Employment, and Prices Paid are the key sub-indices that drive the GDP, labor market, and inflation narratives. Because services represent 68% of GDP and 80% of private employment, these readings have an outsized impact on the macro outlook.
Business Activity ● OUTPUT MEASURE
What it measures: The level of actual business output in the services sector — equivalent to Production in manufacturing. This is the broadest measure of whether the services economy is growing or contracting.

Current reading: 59.9 — Near 60, which is extremely strong. This is the highest since March 2024 and represents a steady acceleration from the mid-2025 soft patch (49.9 in May, 50.0 in Sep). The key drivers are AI/data center related construction and IT investment, healthcare services demand, and broad consumer spending recovery. Construction firms report "pending surge in new capital investments in data centers, combined cycle power, and nuclear market sectors." When Business Activity sustains above 55, it's associated with above-trend GDP growth.

What it means going forward: Near-60 Business Activity signals Q1 2026 GDP is tracking strong. The backlog surge (55.9) provides additional runway, suggesting this isn't a one-month spike but has 2–3 quarters of sustainability. For equity investors, this supports earnings growth in consumer-facing and business services sectors.
New Orders ● LEADING INDICATOR
What it measures: The flow of new business coming into services firms. Like manufacturing, New Orders is the primary leading indicator — it tells you what Business Activity will look like in 1–2 months.

Current reading: 58.6 — A +5.5 point surge from 53.1 in January. This is one of the strongest single-month jumps in recent history. The breadth is key: 14 of 18 sectors reporting growth means this isn't driven by a single industry but reflects broad-based demand. Mining, Information, Real Estate, and Agriculture are leading, while only Retail Trade, Arts & Entertainment, and Transportation are contracting.

What it means going forward: New Orders at 58.6 combined with Order Backlog at 55.9 suggests services output will remain elevated through Q2 2026 at minimum. This level of demand typically leads to further Employment gains as firms struggle to meet demand with existing staff. For GDP, it supports continued strength in the consumer spending component.
Employment ● LABOR MARKET SIGNAL
What it measures: Whether services firms are adding or reducing staff. Given that services employ roughly 80% of the private sector, this is arguably the single most important number in the NMI for the broader economy.

Current reading: 51.8 — Now above 50 for two consecutive months, the highest since June 2024. The recovery from a low of 46.2 in March 2025 has been steady but not dramatic, which is actually a positive sign — it suggests measured, sustainable hiring rather than a hiring frenzy. With Business Activity near 60 and backlogs building, the pressure to add staff will intensify in coming months.

What it means going forward: Services Employment above 50 is bullish for non-farm payrolls, wage growth, and consumer spending. If this sustains above 52–53 for the next 2–3 months, it would signal a genuine labor market re-acceleration and strengthen the consumer spending pillar of GDP. Combined with Manufacturing Employment trending toward 50, the overall employment picture is improving on both sides of the economy.
Prices Paid ● INFLATION SIGNAL
What it measures: Input cost pressures on services firms. Because services account for the majority of core CPI, this reading feeds directly into the inflation narrative and Fed policy decisions.

Current reading: 63.0 — Down from 66.6 in January, a -3.6 point decline and the largest single-month drop in the current cycle. While 63.0 is still elevated (above the long-run average of ~58), the direction of travel is more important than the level. Services inflation had been stubbornly high for 12+ months, and this is the first clear sign of deceleration. Tariff impacts are less direct on services than manufacturing, which explains the divergence.

What it means going forward: If Services Prices continue declining toward 58–60, it would be a major catalyst for Treasury markets (bullish for duration) and strengthen the case for Fed rate cuts in H2 2026. The combination of accelerating growth with decelerating inflation is the textbook "Goldilocks" scenario that equity markets love. However, if tariff-driven goods inflation spills over into services through higher transportation and input costs, this deceleration could stall.
Inventories ● LEADING INDICATOR
What it measures: Whether services firms are building or drawing down inventories. Rising inventories can signal either confidence in future demand (proactive stocking) or weakening sales (involuntary accumulation). Context from New Orders and Business Activity helps distinguish between the two.

Current reading: 56.4 — A dramatic +11.3 point surge from 45.1 in January, the largest single-month swing in the dataset. This follows a volatile pattern — inventories swung from 54.2 in December down to 45.1 in January and now back to 56.4. The context matters: with New Orders at 58.6 and Business Activity at 59.9, this looks like intentional restocking to meet surging demand rather than involuntary accumulation. Firms in Finance & Insurance, Wholesale Trade, and Professional Services are actively building stock to service growing order pipelines.

What it means going forward: Inventories rising alongside strong New Orders is a bullish signal — it means firms expect demand to persist and are investing to meet it. If New Orders were falling while inventories rose, it would be a recession warning. The current configuration supports continued GDP growth and suggests businesses see the demand acceleration as durable, not temporary. Watch for whether inventory builds outpace new orders in coming months, which would signal over-stocking risk.
Supplier Deliveries ● SUPPLY CHAIN SIGNAL
What it measures: The speed at which suppliers deliver goods and services to firms. Above 50 means deliveries are slowing (suppliers can't keep up with demand), while below 50 means deliveries are speeding up (ample supply capacity). Unlike most sub-indices, higher readings here often signal supply constraints rather than economic strength.

Current reading: 53.9 — Deliveries have been gradually slowing since mid-2025, trending upward from 50.3 in April/August to 54.2 in January and 53.9 in February. This is consistent with the broader demand acceleration — when Business Activity is near 60 and New Orders are surging, suppliers naturally face more pressure. The slowing is moderate, not severe (the 2021 supply crisis saw readings above 70), but the trend is worth monitoring given tariff-related disruptions in manufacturing supply chains that can spill over into services.

What it means going forward: Moderately slowing deliveries in a high-demand environment are normal and not yet concerning. However, if tariff impacts on goods and materials start constraining services firms (especially in Construction, Accommodation, and Health Care which depend on physical inputs), deliveries could slow further and become an inflation accelerant. For now, this reading confirms the demand-driven narrative without raising red flags. If deliveries push above 56–58, it would signal genuine bottlenecks forming.
